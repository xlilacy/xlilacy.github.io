title: Space Age
---

My bones grow lighter in zero gravity,
each year dispersing like cosmic dust
while Earth spins below, a marble
of remembered blue.
Through viewport glass, I count
my grey hairs floating—
silver filaments that catch starlight
like antenna receiving signals from home.
The doctors say space ages us faster,
as if we needed the reminder. I watch
my hands grow translucent in artificial light,
mapping new constellations of age spots.
They never told us homesickness
could feel like entropy,
how each orbit pulls another thread
from the fabric of who we were.
My grandchildren's voices crack
across the void, their children's drawings
beam up in pixels. I touch the screen
where their small hands should be.
Mission Control speaks of legacy,
of pushing boundaries, while I dream
of garden soil between my fingers,
of growing old in Earth's sweet gravity.
Even heroes tire of floating.
Some nights I press my feet against the wall,
pretending it's solid ground,
pretending I'm walking home.
